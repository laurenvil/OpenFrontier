History Study Notes - The Industrial Revolution

The Industrial Revolution began in Great Britain in the late 18th century, around 1760. It marked a dramatic shift from hand-production methods to machine-based manufacturing.

Key inventions that drove the revolution:
- The Spinning Jenny (1764) by James Hargreaves - allowed one worker to operate multiple spindles
- The Steam Engine improvements by James Watt (1769) - provided power for factories and transportation
- The Power Loom (1785) by Edmund Cartwright - mechanized the weaving process

Coal became the primary fuel source during this period. It powered steam engines in factories and locomotives, replacing wood and water as the main energy sources.

The revolution led to massive urbanization as workers moved from rural farms to cities seeking factory jobs. Cities like Manchester and Birmingham grew rapidly. Working conditions were often harsh, with 12-16 hour days, low pay, and widespread child labor.

The factory system replaced the cottage industry model. Instead of craftsmen working at home, workers gathered in large factories where production could be standardized and supervised. This increased output dramatically but reduced worker autonomy.

Transportation was transformed by the steam locomotive. The first public railway, the Liverpool and Manchester Railway, opened in 1830. This revolutionized trade by making it faster and cheaper to move goods across the country.
